I am a Ph.D. candidate in Economics at Texas Tech University, specializing in applied microeconomics, industrial organization, and health economics. My research examines how information frictions, consumer behavior, and public policy shape health and market outcomes. In particular, I study how nutritional information, climate-related shocks, and market accessibility influence consumer decisions and welfare. My current research explores topics such as front-of-package sugar labeling, retail discrimination against mobility-constrained consumers, and the effects of extreme temperatures on sugar consumption.

Methodologically, my work combines reduced-form econometric techniques with structural modeling to identify causal relationships and evaluate policy interventions. I employ methods including difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, high-dimensional fixed effects, and structural demand estimation using Berry-Levinsohn-Pakes (BLP) models augmented with rational inattention frameworks. By integrating behavioral economics with industrial organization, my research seeks to understand how information-processing costs affect consumer choices and to design policies that improve health and economic welfare.

Prior to beginning my Ph.D., I earned both an M.Phil. and a B.A. in Economics from Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology (KNUST) in Ghana, where I graduated with distinction and received multiple awards for academic excellence. Before joining Texas Tech, I served as a teaching and research assistant at KNUST, supporting instruction in microeconomics, econometrics, and environmental economics, while also gaining experience in survey management and data analysis. I have additionally worked in the banking sector, conducting risk analysis and developing analytical tools at Access Bank Ghana.

My research has been published in journals including *Studies in Nonlinear Dynamics & Econometrics*, *Applied Economics*, and the *Journal of Developing Areas*. I have presented my work at major conferences such as the ASSA/AEA Annual Meetings and the Midwest Economic Association Conference. I have also received several honors, including the Dr. Rashid Al-Hmoud Fellowship at Texas Tech University and the Best Economics Student Award at KNUST. In addition to my research activities, I currently serve as an instructor for Principles of Microeconomics at Texas Tech University.
